We Refugees

I come from a musical place
Where they shoot me for my song
And my brother has been tortured
By my brother in my land.

I come from a beautiful place
Where they hate my shade of skin
They don’t like the way I pray
And they ban free poetry.

I come from a beautiful place
Where girls cannot go to school
There you are told what to believe
And even young boys must grow beards.

I come from a great old forest
I think it is now a field
And the people I once knew
Are not there now.

We can all be refugees
Nobody is safe,
All it takes is a mad leader
Or no rain to bring forth food,
We can all be refugees
We can all be told to go,
We can be hated by someone
For being someone.

I come from a beautiful place
Where the valley floods each year
And each year the hurricane tells us
That we must keep moving on.

I come from an ancient place
All my family were born there
And I would like to go there
But I really want to live.

I come from a sunny, sandy place
Where tourists go to darken skin
And dealers like to sell guns there
I just can’t tell you what’s the price.

I am told I have no country now
I am told I am a lie
I am told that modern history books
May forget my name.

We can all be refugees
Sometimes it only takes a day,
Sometimes it only takes a handshake
Or a paper that is signed.
We all came from refugees
Nobody simply just appeared,
Nobody’s here without a struggle,
And why should we live in fear
Of the weather or the troubles?
We all came here from somewhere.

Benjamin Zephaniah

Dr. Benjamin Zephaniah, born in Birmingham, England, is a poet, musician, and activist known for blending Jamaican Dub Poetry with political themes. Leaving school at 13, he gained local fame in Handsworth before moving to London, where his first book, Pen Rhythm, was published.

In the 1980s, he became a key voice in protests against racism, police brutality, and social injustice, using poetry to reach mainstream audiences. He later performed worldwide, collaborated with The Wailers in tribute to Nelson Mandela, and mixed Reggae, Hip Hop, and Jazz in his music.

His children’s poetry (Talking Turkeys) and teen novels (Face) tackled real-world issues, inspiring young writers. With 16 honorary doctorates, a hospital wing named after him, and a global impact, Zephaniah remains dedicated to activism, poetry, and human rights.

All the Sustainable Development Goals of the Agend 2030

This poem addresses several themes connected to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) of the 2030 Agenda.

Here are the most relevant goals:

No Poverty

  • The poem describes refugees forced to leave their homes due to war, persecution, or environmental disasters.
  • The lines "We can all be refugees, nobody is safe" and "Sometimes it only takes a handshake or a paper that is signed" emphasize how quickly people can lose everything.

Zero Hunger

  • The line "Or no rain to bring forth food" points to food insecurity caused by drought.
  • The poem highlights how people are forced to move when they can no longer grow food due to environmental conditions.

Quality Education

  • The line "Where girls cannot go to school" directly addresses the lack of access to education, particularly for girls in certain regions.
  • Education is a key factor in breaking cycles of poverty and oppression, but many children, especially girls, are denied this right.

Gender Equality

  • The poem references restrictions placed on girls and women, such as "Where girls cannot go to school"
  • In some places, cultural or political forces prevent women from accessing education, work, or personal freedom.
  • Gender discrimination is deeply tied to poverty, violence, and lack of opportunities.

Reduced Inequalities

  • The poem discusses racial and religious discrimination: "Where they hate my shade of skin / They don’t like the way I pray."
  • The line "I am told I have no country now / I am told I am a lie" reflects the harsh reality of stateless people and those whose identities are denied.

Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions

  • The poem speaks about violence and oppression: "They shoot me for my song / And my brother has been tortured."
  • Political persecution, war, and weak justice systems force people to flee their countries.
